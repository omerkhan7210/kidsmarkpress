Honest advice — from experience

Suggestions & things worth knowing before you start

These are things most developers won't tell you because they either don't know them yet or don't want to complicate the sale. I'm sharing them because they'll save you money, frustration, and rebuilds down the road.
Start with a content-first mindset
The most common reason publishing sites feel empty at launch: the site is built before the content exists. Before Day 1, I'd recommend having at least 3–5 books ready to populate the catalog, 3–5 author profiles, and 5–10 mentor tributes pre-seeded (you can submit them yourself as examples). A site with real content on launch day performs better in search engines, looks more credible to visitors, and gives your team a working template to follow when adding more.
The Mentor Wall will get spam — plan for it
Any public submission form that doesn't require login will attract bot submissions. reCAPTCHA helps, but admin moderation is your real protection — which is already built into this scope. My honest suggestion: designate one person on your team who checks the moderation queue at least twice a week. If it sits unattended for weeks, approved tributes will back up and the public wall will feel stale. This is a people process, not just a technical one.
Don't skip Phase 2 for too long
The Author Submission Portal (Phase 2) is actually one of your core stated objectives — "allow potential authors to submit information for publication consideration." Right now in Phase 1, there's no formal way for prospective authors to submit to you beyond the contact form. If receiving author submissions is important from launch, consider fast-tracking the Author Submission Portal into Phase 1 for a modest addition to the budget, rather than sending serious authors to a generic contact form.
On the $500 budget
I want to be direct: $500 will get you a pre-made theme with your logo dropped in — no database-driven catalog, no searchable directories, no moderation workflow. That's not what your RFP is asking for. The price in this proposal ($1,800 for Phase 1) is already well below US-market rate for this feature set. I'm pricing it this way because the project is interesting and I want the work — but I'd rather tell you that honestly now than cut corners to hit $500 and hand you something that doesn't actually work the way you need it to.
Invest in a good newsletter tool early
The newsletter signup on the home page is included, but which tool you connect it to matters. MailPoet is free up to 1,000 subscribers and lives inside WordPress (no third-party dashboard to learn). Mailchimp is the industry standard and integrates with everything but has a steeper learning curve. My recommendation for a team new to email marketing: start with MailPoet, migrate to Mailchimp when you hit 1,000 subscribers and need more automation power.
SEO won't happen overnight — start blogging from Day 1
Rank Math will be configured and all pages will be SEO-ready at launch, but search engine rankings take 3–6 months minimum to build. The single biggest thing you can do to accelerate this: publish one blog post per week from launch day, specifically targeting search terms your authors and readers would actually type — "how to get a children's book published," "mentor recognition ideas," "independent publishing for first-time authors." The blog section is built for this. Use it early and consistently.
